Why this topic matters

A guide that speaks to real life, not just theory.

Many young people learn about money only after making stressful mistakes: overspending, borrowing too quickly, trusting suspicious offers, or treating financial pressure as normal. Our project responds to that gap with a youth-focused guide that is visual, interactive, and easy to apply in everyday life.

Psychology of financial literacy

Money decisions are often emotional before they become logical.

This section can connect your guide to the psychologist collaboration by showing how beliefs, inner conflict, impulsivity, and stress influence financial behavior.

Impulsive mode

“I want it now.” Spending becomes emotional relief, reward, or escape from discomfort.

Fearful mode

“I never have enough.” Saving may become anxiety-driven rather than healthy and balanced.

Critical mode

“You should not spend at all.” Extreme restriction can create guilt, shame, and later rebound spending.

Balanced mode

Pause, check facts, and decide from a calmer state. This is the habit the guide tries to strengthen.

SLC journey

How this website can document the whole project

This section is designed to help you present the 10 SLC challenges clearly, with actions, evidence, impact, and next steps.

Challenge 1 — Clarify Values

Present your team values such as responsibility, honesty, empathy, prevention, and long-term thinking.

  • What to show: values poster, manifesto, or visual identity
  • Why it matters: it explains the foundation of your project decisions
  • Evidence ideas: value cards, design drafts, discussion notes, team agreement

Challenge 2 — Set a Personal Example

Show how team members modeled healthy money behavior, responsible planning, or active contribution.

  • What to show: personal actions, volunteer work, planning, content creation
  • Why it matters: leadership becomes visible through action, not claims
  • Evidence ideas: screenshots, production work, behind-the-scenes contributions

Challenge 3 — Share a Vision

Describe a future where young adults make calmer, safer, and more informed financial decisions.

  • What to show: your vision statement and audience-centered future scene
  • Why it matters: it makes the project inspiring instead of only descriptive
  • Evidence ideas: vision page, slogan, campaign headline, concept sketches

Challenge 4 — Enlist Others

Document how you used websites, posters, social posts, or events to attract attention and support.

  • What to show: outreach materials and audience interaction
  • Why it matters: support and engagement demonstrate real communication
  • Evidence ideas: posters, QR codes, page links, comments, view counts

Challenge 5 — Search for Opportunities

Highlight your research, expert interview work, stakeholder outreach, and the gaps you identified.

  • What to show: interview with an expert, findings, audience needs, risks
  • Why it matters: this proves depth, not just surface-level ideas
  • Evidence ideas: interview links, notes, summary cards, question lists

Challenge 6 — Experiment and Take Risks

Show how you turned research into action through a creative financial literacy initiative or event.

  • What to show: campaign concept, interactive site, audience activity, outcomes
  • Why it matters: this is where the project becomes initiative-driven
  • Evidence ideas: event pages, participation records, photos, results, reflections

Challenge 7 — Foster Collaboration

Feature collaboration with a psychologist or another relevant expert to deepen the project’s impact.

  • What to show: psychology meeting, guest session, Q&A, prize activity, and shared insights
  • Why it matters: external collaboration strengthens credibility and reach
  • Evidence ideas: presentation slides, event photos, attendance, prizes, feedback

Challenge 8 — Strengthen Others

Use this guide itself as evidence that you empowered peers with tools, reflection prompts, and practical advice.

  • What to show: downloadable guide, checklist, workshop activity, audience learning
  • Why it matters: it proves your project helped others grow
  • Evidence ideas: testimonials, quiz use, downloads, skill-based activities

Challenge 9 — Recognize Contributions

Thank teammates, guest speakers, participants, and supporters who helped move the project forward.

  • What to show: acknowledgment board or appreciation section
  • Why it matters: it demonstrates team culture and leadership maturity
  • Evidence ideas: names, roles, thank-you messages, contribution highlights

Challenge 10 — Celebrate Values and Victories

Close with outcomes, lessons, achievements, and the value of continuing this work beyond one project.

  • What to show: final impact summary and team reflection
  • Why it matters: it turns your project into a complete leadership story
  • Evidence ideas: final montage, summary numbers, reflections, audience response
